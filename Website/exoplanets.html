Exoplanets are planets that exist beyond our own solar system. Most exoplanets orbit other stars but some, called "Rogue Planets", orbit the center of the galaxy of which they're a part and do not orbit any star.

They are fascinating for several reasons! There are some exoplanets that are similar to earth and could even host extraterrestrial life, some that have glass rain, and even some whoose surface is covered in molten lava. The possibilities are endless. (Hover over the images to read more about them)

Kepler-453b
This planet is an earth-like rocky planet orbiting a star similar to the sun within the star's habitable zone. It is commonly called Earth 2.0
Corot-7b
A planet that orbits so close to a star, that its surface is covered in molten lava, while having high geographical activity due to the strong gravitational forces on it.
HD 189733 b
A hot deep blue planet about 65 light-years away with molten glass rain. It was also the first planet to have its thermal map constructed.
PSR B1620-26 b
The oldest known exoplanet with an estimated age of 12.7 billion, It is also the first ever planet to be confirmed to orbit two stars!

Exoplanets have become an important area of astrophysics in the last two decades. Important methods used to detect exoplanets are direct imaging, astrometry, radial velocity, transit event observation, and microlensing. Scientists are actively working on the project Breakthrough Starshot, which aims to engineer centimeter-sized sails that are capable of travelling at 20% the speed of light to travel to distant solar systems and communicate data back to us on earth.

Alpha Centauri Cb
This is an earth-like planet which orbits Proxima Centauri b, a star only 4 light years away. In the future we could explore it using a light sail called starchip
